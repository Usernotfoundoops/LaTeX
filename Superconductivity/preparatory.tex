\section{Introduction}
In 1902, Lord Kelvin raised a question that would shape low-temperature physics 
for the next decade: what happens to the electrical resistance of a metal as 
temperature approaches absolute zero? Would the reduced thermal motion of lattice 
ions eventually allow electrons to pass without scattering, driving resistance to 
zero, or would the electrons themselves freeze, sending resistance toward infinity? \cite{vanDelft2010}.
Neither outcome could be tested without access to temperatures 
far below anything achievable at the time.

This changed on 10 July 1908, when Kamerlingh Onnes successfully liquefied helium, 
opening up temperatures below 14\,K for the first time \cite{vanDelft2010}. After 
early attempts with platinum were hampered by instrumentation limitations, \textbf{mercury} 
was selected as the test material due to the high purity achievable by distillation.
It was later found that purity played no significant role in the onset of superconductivity,
 as the resistance vanished identically in pure mercury and impure amalgams alike, with lead and tin also found to be superconductors shortly after, at transition temperatures near 7.2\,K and 3.7\,K respectively. 
On 8 April 1911, cooling mercury below 4.2\,K caused its resistance to drop so 
sharply it fell below the detection limit of the apparatus entirely 
\cite{vanDelft2010, Buckel2004}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.38\textwidth]{figures/Onnes_Actutal_Hg.png}
    \caption{Ultra-cooled mercury showing nearly zero resistance\cite{vanDelft2010}.}
    \label{fig:RvsT_Hg}
\end{figure}

To quantify the effect, Onnes induced persistent currents in closed \textbf{superconducting 
rings} and monitored them over time. Any finite resistance $R$ would cause 
exponential decay as
\begin{equation}
    I(t) = I_0 \, e^{-\frac{R}{L}t}
    \label{eq:decay}
\end{equation}

%No such decay was observed, bounding the resistance to at least 14 orders of 
%magnitude below that of copper at 300\,K, a difference comparable to that between 
%a conductor and an insulator \cite{Buckel2004}.
In 1933, Meissner and Ochsenfeld 
further established that superconductors expel magnetic flux entirely from their 
interior upon cooling below $T_c$, identifying ideal diamagnetism as a second and 
independent defining property of the superconducting state \cite{Buckel2004}. 

These two phenomena form the experimental basis of this report, in which the 
superconducting transition of \ch{YBa2Cu3O7} and Bi$_2$Sr$_2$CaCu$_2$O$_{8+x}$ samples
is characterised through \textbf{resistive four-point measurements} and inductive 
AC-susceptibility measurements, alongside a demonstration of the 
Meissner-Ochsenfeld effect through magnetic levitation \cite{experiment}.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
Superconductivity is characterised by the complete vanishing of electrical 
resistance below a material-specific critical temperature, $T_c$, at which 
point a persistent current can flow indefinitely without dissipation. 
Unlike an ordinary metallic conductor, whose resistance decreases only 
gradually as temperature is lowered, a superconductor undergoes an \textbf{abrupt 
drop to zero resistance at $T_c$} (see Figure \ref{fig:RvsT_Hg}). The mechanisms governing 
electrical transport differ fundamentally between metals, semiconductors, 
and superconductors, and each class exhibits a qualitatively distinct 
$R(T)$ behaviour. The microscopic basis for superconducting behaviour is 
provided by BCS theory (Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer, 1957), which 
describes the formation of Cooper pairs through phonon-mediated 
electron - electron attractions, producing a dissipation-free quantum 
superfluid that carries current without resistance \cite{wiki_super}.


\section{Electrical Resistance and Temperature Dependence}
\subsection{Metals}
In a metal, electrical conduction is carried by a gas of free electrons 
occupying states up to the Fermi energy $E_F$. In the \textbf{Drude model}, a 
constant electric field accelerates electrons between collisions, and the 
resulting steady-state current density gives the conductivity \cite{Kittel2005}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma = \frac{ne^2\tau}{m_e}
    \label{eq:drude}
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the free electron density, $e$ is the electron charge, $m_e$ 
is the electron mass, and $\tau$ is the mean time between scattering events. 
The electrical resistivity follows as 
\begin{equation}
    \rho = 1/\sigma = m_e/ne^2\tau
    \label{eq:resistivity}
\end{equation}

Two scattering mechanisms contribute independently to the total resistivity 
\cite{Kittel2005}. The first arises from thermally excited lattice vibrations 
(phonons), which become stronger with increasing temperature. The 
second comes from static crystal imperfections such as impurities and 
vacancies, which are temperature-independent. These two contributions add independently according to \textbf{Matthiessen's rule}
\begin{equation}
    \rho(T) = \rho_{\text{L}}(T) + \rho_{\text{i}}
    \label{eq:matthiessen}
\end{equation}


where $\rho_{\text{i}}$ is the residual resistivity at $T \to 0$, reflecting 
the purity and structural quality of the sample \cite{Kittel2005}. At high 
temperatures $\rho_L \propto T$, where $\rho_L$ is the phonon contribution. So resistance increases linearly. As 
$T \to 0$, phonon scattering freezes out.

\subsection{Semiconductors}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------

In a semiconductor, the valence and conduction bands are separated by an 
energy gap $E_g$ of order 1\,eV \cite{Kittel2005}. At zero temperature no 
free carriers exist and the material is effectively insulating. As 
temperature increases, electrons are \textbf{thermally excited across the gap}, 
generating mobile electrons in the conduction band and holes in the valence 
band, both of which contribute to conduction. The intrinsic carrier concentration grows exponentially with temperature 
as 
\begin{equation} n_i \propto T^{3/2}\exp\!\left(\frac{-E_g}{2k_BT}\right) \end{equation}
and since this exponential factor dominates, the resistivity decreases with increasing temperature 
\cite{Kittel2005}

\begin{equation}
    \rho(T) \propto \exp\!\left(\frac{E_g}{2k_BT}\right)
    \label{eq:sc_rho}
\end{equation}

This is the opposite behaviour to metals. Since \textbf{$\sigma = 1/\rho$}, one 
equivalently plots $\ln\sigma$ against $1/T$ (refer Fig. \ref{fig:sc_RT}b), which yields a straight line 
with slope $-E_g/2k_B$, providing a direct way to extract the band gap from 
conductance measurements \cite{Kittel2005}.

\subsection{Superconductors and BCS Theory}



Above $T_c$ a superconductor behaves as a normal metal. Below $T_c$, 
however, the resistance drops abruptly to zero, which cannot be explained 
by reduced phonon scattering alone. The \textbf{Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS)} 
theory provides the microscopic explanation: a phonon-mediated 
attractive interaction between electrons near the Fermi surface causes them 
to form bound pairs known as Cooper pairs \cite{Tinkham1996}. Each Cooper 
pair consists of two electrons with equal and opposite momenta and opposite 
spins. For instance, if one-particle orbital is occupied by a spin up electron $\uparrow $ and wavevector \textbf{k} then the other is occupied by a spin down electron $\downarrow$ with wavevector $-\textbf{k}$. If the orbital is vacant then its vice versa. \cite{Kittel2005}.

All Cooper pairs condense into the same macroscopic quantum ground state, 
described by a single wave function with a well-defined phase 
\cite{Buckel2004}. This condensate is separated from excited states by an 
energy gap $\Delta$. A thermal fluctuation cannot scatter the condensate 
\textbf{unless it supplies enough energy to break a Cooper pair}, which requires 
$k_BT \sim \Delta$. Below $T_c$ this condition is not met, so scattering 
is suppressed entirely and resistance vanishes \cite{Tinkham1996}.


\begin{equation}
    2\Delta(0) = 3.52\, k_B T_c
    \label{eq:bcs_gap_ratio}
\end{equation}
\newpage
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{figures/BCS.png}
    \caption{Temperature dependence of the $\Delta$ \cite{Szab_1994}.}
    \label{fig:bcs_gap}
\end{figure}

The BCS theory predicts a universal relation between the zero-temperature 
gap and the critical temperature. Experimental values across many materials typically fall in the range $3.0\,k_BT_c$ to $4.5\,k_BT_c$, in good agreement with the BCS prediction \cite{Tinkham1996}.



\section{Magnetic Properties of Superconductors}

\subsection{Ideal Diamagnetism and the Meissner Effect}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.6\textwidth}
        \centering \includegraphics[width=0.99\textwidth]{figures/MeissnerFX.png}
        \caption{Demonstration of the Meissner-Ochsenfeld effect by dropping a permanent magnet onto a lead bowl. Left: $T>T_c$, right: $T<T_c$. Adapted from Buckel and Kleiner \cite{Buckel2004}.}
        \label{fig:meissner}
    \end{minipage}\hfill
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.33\textwidth}    
        \centering \includegraphics[width=0.61\textwidth]{figures/Diamagnetism.png}
        \caption{Magnetic field lines around a superconductor showing diamagnetic behavior \cite{Tinkham1996}.}
        \label{fig:diamagnetism}
    \end{minipage}
\end{figure}
A superconductor cooled below its critical temperature $T_c$ exhibits 
perfect diamagnetism, spontaneously expelling all internal magnetic flux 
in what is known as the \textbf{Meissner-Ochsenfeld effect}, first observed by 
Meissner and Ochsenfeld in 1933 \cite{Buckel2004}. The external field 
does not vanish abruptly at the surface but instead penetrates a thin 
surface layer over a characteristic length scale known as the London 
penetration depth, defined as

\begin{equation}
    \lambda_L = \sqrt{\frac{m}{\mu_0 q^2 n_s}},
    \label{eq:london_depth}
\end{equation}

where $m$ is the carrier mass, $q$ the charge, $n_s$ the superfluid 
density, and $\mu_0$ the permeability of free space. Beyond this surface 
layer the field decays exponentially as

\begin{equation}
    B(x) = B(0)\exp\!\left(-\frac{x}{\lambda_L}\right),
    \label{eq:field_decay}
\end{equation}

becoming negligibly small deep within the bulk. It is worth noting that 
this behaviour is fundamentally different from that of a perfect classical 
conductor, and can only be understood within a quantum mechanical 
framework \cite{Buckel2004, Tinkham1996}.



\subsection{Type-I vs. Type-II Superconductors}

Type-I superconductors are characterised by a \textbf{single critical field} $H_c$, 
below which ideal diamagnetism is maintained and above which 
superconductivity is abruptly destroyed; elemental superconductors such 
as mercury and lead fall into this category \cite{Buckel2004, Tinkham1996}. 
Type-II superconductors instead exhibit \textbf{two critical fields}, $H_{c1}$ and 
$H_{c2}$, and are distinguished by a Ginzburg-Landau parameter
\begin{equation}
    \kappa = \frac{\lambda_L}{\xi_{GL}} > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},
    \label{eq:GL_param}
\end{equation}

where $\xi_{GL}$ is the Ginzburg-Landau coherence length, which gives 
rise to a negative surface energy between normal and superconducting 
regions \cite{abrikosov2003, Tinkham1996}. Between $H_{c1}$ and 
$H_{c2}$, the material enters the Shubnikov phase, in which \textbf{magnetic 
flux partially penetrates the sample as discrete flux lines} while zero 
electrical resistance is maintained \cite{Buckel2004}. Notably, virtually 
all superconducting alloys and compounds discovered since the 1960s, 
including all known high-temperature superconductors where $H_{c2}$ can 
exceed 100~T, belong to the Type-II class \cite{abrikosov2003, Buckel2004}.

\subsection{Flux Quantization and the Abrikosov Vortex Lattice}
In the Shubnikov phase, magnetic flux does not penetrate uniformly but 
is instead confined to discrete quantised tubes, each carrying exactly 
one magnetic flux quantum

\begin{equation}
    \Phi_0 = \frac{h}{2e} \approx 2.07 \times 10^{-15} \text{ T m}^2,
    \label{eq:flux_quantum}
\end{equation}

as confirmed experimentally by Doll and Nabauer, and independently by 
Deaver and Fairbank, in 1961 \cite{Buckel2004}. Each vortex consists 
of a normal-state core of radius $\xi_{GL}$, which physically represents 
the spatial extent of a Cooper pair, surrounded by circulating 
supercurrents that decay over the length scale $\lambda_L$ \cite{Tinkham1996}. 
The lower and upper critical fields can be estimated as

\begin{equation}
    H_{c1} \approx \frac{\Phi_0}{\pi\lambda_L^2}, \qquad
    H_{c2} \approx \frac{\Phi_0}{\pi\xi_{GL}^2},
    \label{eq:critical_fields}
\end{equation}

showing that a \textbf{smaller coherence length gives rise to a substantially 
larger upper critical field}. Using Ginzburg-Landau theory, Abrikosov 
showed that these vortices arrange into a periodic triangular lattice 
that minimises the free energy of the mixed state, a prediction later 
confirmed through neutron diffraction and iron-colloid decoration 
experiments \cite{abrikosov2003, Buckel2004}.

\section{Magnetic Susceptibility and Magnetization}

In a magnetic field, the magnetisation M of a system is the magnetic moment per unit volume. This is related to the applied magnetic field H linearly by the dimensionless factor magnetic susceptibility, i.e $M = \chi H$\cite{Simon:1581455}\footnotemark \footnotetext{H: Magnetic Field Strength (A/m); B: Magnetic Flux Density (T)}.
The magnetisation and magnetic field relate to each other as:
\begin{equation}
    B = \mu_0 (H + M)
\end{equation}
For small susceptibilities, this can be written as
\begin{equation}
    M = \frac{\chi B}{\mu_0}.
\end{equation}

Here, a \textbf{paramagnet} is a substance which has positive susceptibility ($\chi > 0$) and their magnetisation aligns with the applied magnetic field. A \textbf{diamagnet} has negative susceptibility ($\chi < 0$) and its magnetisation is in the opposite direction as the applied field.\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.40\textwidth]{figures/magnetisation curves - para, dia, ferro.png}
    \caption{Magnetization curves showing the $M$ vs $H$ relationships for paramagnetic, diamagnetic, and ferromagnetic materials.\cite{LAU2022301}}
    \label{fig:magnetisation_curves}
\end{figure} There are no unpaired electrons and the net magnetic moment is zero\cite{Simon:1581455}. Type I and II superconductors both exhibit perfect diamagnetism since the magnetic fields are expelled from the interior, this results in a magnetic susceptibility of -1. \textbf{Ferromagnetic} materials are similar to paramagnetic materials, but its magnetisation M is non-zero even without an applied magnetic field and is much stronger. It can also be called spontaneous magnetism\cite{Simon:1581455}. The susceptibilities of these different materials are visualised in \textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:magnetisation_curves}}.

\subsection{Critical Field, Temperature, and Current Density}

Type-I superconductors have a single critical magnetic field $H_{c}$ point beyond which beyond which superconductivity is lost entirely. Type-II superconductors are characterised by two critical fields, $H_{c1}$ and $H_{c2}$. Below $H_{c1}$, the material exhibits perfect diamagnetism as in the Meissner state. 
Between the \textbf{two critical fields}, magnetic flux penetrates the material in the form of 
quantised vortices, forming the mixed (Shubnikov) phase, where perfect diamagnetism is 
no longer maintained\cite{Chaddah2003}. Above $H_{c2}$, superconductivity breaks down entirely. In addition 
to the critical fields, superconducting behaviour is restricted to \textbf{temperatures below $T_c$}, 
since thermal energy above this point is sufficient to destroy the Cooper pairs responsible 
for the condensate. A \textbf{critical current density} $J_c$ also plays an important role: when the 
applied current exceeds $J_c$, the Lorentz force on the Abrikosov vortices overcomes the 
pinning forces from lattice defects, setting the vortices in motion\cite{Chaddah2003}. Moving vortices induce 
an electric field parallel to $\mathbf{J}$, leading to a finite \textit{flux-flow resistance} 
and energy dissipation even within the mixed state\cite{Chaddah2003}. Perfect conductivity is therefore only 
sustained as long as the current remains below $J_c$.

For this experiment, a small sample of the Bi$_2$Sr$_2$CaCu$_2$O$_{8+x}$ is removed and fixed on to a sapphire substrate. Four gold contacts are fixed on to the substrate for which thermal evaporation is used here. \textbf{Thin aluminium wires} are used for conducting electricity between the gold contacts and the prepared sample. A constant current is passed through the sample and the voltage between the contacts $U_{4p}$ is obtained\cite{experiment}. Using a voltmeter of a high internal resistance, one can measure only the small voltage drop between the contacts. Using \textbf{Ohm's law}, the resistance can be calculated due to its proportionality with the voltage. If a 2-point method is used, the voltages at the contacts is also measured and results in errors\cite{experiment}.

The voltage and resistance are then measured at different temperatures by both cooling and heating the sample to produce a hysteresis curve.

\subsection{Inductive AC-Susceptibility Measurements}\label{sec:ac_susceptibility}
These measurements enables one to find the \textbf{critical temperature $T_c$} and the width of the phase transition $\Delta T_c$. A mutual inductance bridge with three induction coils is used for this measurement\cite{experiment}. Two secondary coils are used here which are identical to each other and have equal windings. The first one (reference coil) only experiences the field from the primary coil. Besides this field, the other secondary coil (sample coil) also experiences the field from the superconducting sample. The secondary coils are contained inside the primary coil as shown in Figure \ref{fig:solenoid}. 

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering \includegraphics[width=0.47\textwidth]{figures/Expt Part/Inductive measurement - primary and secondary coil setup.png}
    \caption{Mutual inductance solenoid setup using primary and secondary coils.\cite{experiment}}
    \label{fig:solenoid}
\end{figure}


The application of an alternating current through the primary coil causes an alternating magnetic field inside it. Due to this alternating behaviour, the complex susceptibility and the time dependent magnetic field have to be considered. The external magnetic field follows the form:

\begin{equation}
H_a = H_0 + h_{AC} \cos(\omega t)
\end{equation}

The induced voltage is given by the equation:

\begin{equation}
U = \pi r^2 \mu_0 \left( h_{AC} \omega \sin(\omega t) - \frac{dM}{dt} \right)
\end{equation}

where $M$ is the magnetic momentum, $r$ is the radius of the secondary sample coil, $\sin(\omega t)$ represents the induction signal of the alternating field, and $h_{AC}$ is the amplitude of the alternating field

The magnetic momentum can be written as a Fourier series since it is periodic:
\begin{equation}
M = \langle M \rangle_T + h_{AC} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \chi_n' \cos(n\omega t) + \chi_n'' \sin(n\omega t) \right)
\end{equation}

where $\chi_n'$ and $\chi_n''$ are the \textbf{real and imaginary components} of the magnetic susceptibility. They are written as:
\begin{equation}
\chi_n' = \frac{\omega}{\pi h_{AC}} \int_{0}^{T} M \cos(n\omega t) \, dt
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\chi_n'' = \frac{\omega}{\pi h_{AC}} \int_{0}^{T} M \sin(n\omega t) \, dt
\end{equation}

Plugging in these terms into the induced voltage equation yields:
\begin{equation}
U = U_0 \left[ \sin(\omega t) + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n \left( \chi_n' \sin(n\omega t) - \chi_n'' \cos(n\omega t) \right) \right]
\end{equation}

Using a lock-in amplifier, the $n=1$ term is isolated from the magnetic momentum. Here, the $\sin$ term dominates over the whole induction signal so it has to be removed. This is done so by combining the reference and sample coils in series with opposite windings.
\begin{equation}
U_1^* = U_0 \left( \chi_1' \sin(\omega t) - \chi_1'' \cos(\omega t) \right) \label{sus_imandre}
\end{equation}

The real and imaginary parts of the susceptibility refer to the \textbf{inductive and resistive components}, respectively. The area enclosed by the hysteresis curve of the magnetic flux density is given as:
\begin{equation}
A_H = \oint \mu_0 H_\alpha \, dB = \int_{0}^{T} \mu_0 \left( H_0 + h_{AC} \cos(\omega t) \right) \frac{dB}{dt} \, dt 
\end{equation}

The area relates to $\chi''$ as: $A_H = \pi (\mu_0 h_{AC})^2 \chi''$. For normally conducting samples, both components of susceptibility tend to zero, so $U_1^* \approx 0$. For a superconducting sample in the Meissner phase, $\chi' \approx -1$ and $\chi'' \approx 0$, therefore the hysteresis curve is not seen. For type II superconductors, $\chi'$ changes while $\chi'' \neq 0$\cite{experiment}.

The deep stick/dipstick is used when handling samples at low-temperature environments to place them in a cryostat. The sample is placed in a chamber at the end of the dipstick and it can help insert samples in a \textbf{liquid helium bath}. Dipsticks can be combined with temperature control devices and diodes for measurement. It allows for the study of the electrical and thermal properties at cryogenic temperatures\cite{badenhorst2008cryogenic}. 
\subsubsection{Lock-In Technique}
Lock-in amplifiers are useful to extract signals in extremely noisy environments, even extremely weak signals million times lower in amplitude than the noise. The input is multiplied by a \textbf{reference signal} which is either provided externally or generated by the lock-in amplifier itself. An \textbf{adjustable low-pass} filter is applied to this result, isolating the signal at the required frequency . Frequencies higher than the reference are filtered out\cite{zurich2016lockin}.

\section{Analysis of Experimental Methods}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Normal-State Behaviour and Hysteresis}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

Above the superconducting transition, \ch{YBa2Cu3O7} exhibits conventional 
metallic behaviour: the resistance increases monotonically with temperature, 
consistent with electron--phonon scattering dominating normal-state 
transport~\cite{Tinkham1996}. This near-linear dependence serves as the 
reference baseline for the linear extrapolation analysis below.

A key systematic error arises due to the difference in thermal inertia between the thermometer and the sample. Since the rate of temperature change is 
governed by~\cite{cengel2014}
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dT}{dt} = \frac{\dot{Q}}{m c_p},
\end{equation}

the Pt100 thermometer having small and of low heat capacity responds differently than the ceramic sample based on the cycle. On \textit{cooling}, the sensor temperature drops faster, 
reading lower than the true sample temperature and shifting the apparent 
transition downward. On \textit{heating}, the sensor warms faster, but 
the sweep rate is slower and thermal equilibrium is better maintained. 
Additionally, the anomalous peak of \ch{YBa2Cu3O7} at 
$T_c$~\cite{junod1990} causes the sample to absorb extra heat precisely 
at the transition, further slowing the sample temperature change and 
promoting sensor-sample equilibration.

The resistance drops at a higher apparent temperature on cooling than it

cycles described above. Due to this, the heating curve with smaller uncertainties is preferred for quantitative analysis.

\subsection{Determination of Critical Temperatures}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

The critical temperatures $T_{c,\mathrm{onset}}$ and $T_{c,0}$ are 
extracted via linear extrapolation: straight lines are fitted to the 
normal state, the gradual part of the transition,the steepest part of the transition, and the superconducting 
state, with adjacent intersections defining the critical 
temperatures~\cite{Montgomery2012LinearRegression}. For a fit of the form
%
\begin{equation}
    R(T) = m\,T + b,
\end{equation}
%
where $m$ is the slope, $b$ is the offset, and $T$ is the temperature measured by the sensor. The intersection of two lines 
$R = m_1 T + b_1$ and $R = m_2 T + b_2$ gives~\cite{bevington2003}
%
\begin{equation}
    T_c = \frac{b_2 - b_1}{m_1 - m_2},
\end{equation}
%
with propagated uncertainty given by
%
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{T_c} = \frac{1}{\left|m_1 - m_2\right|}
    \sqrt{\sigma_{b_1}^2 + \sigma_{b_2}^2 
          + \left(\frac{b_2 - b_1}{m_1 - m_2}\right)^{\!2}
            \!\left(\sigma_{m_1}^2 + \sigma_{m_2}^2\right)} \label{eq:uncertainty}
\end{equation}

the uncertainties $\sigma_{b_1}$, $\sigma_{b_2}$, $\sigma_{m_1}$, and $\sigma_{m_2}$ are obtained from the linear regression fit using scipy's \texttt{curve\_fit} function.

\subsection{Superconducting Fluctuations at the Phase Transition}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

Below $T_{c,0}$, long-range phase coherence is fully established and hole-like 
charge carriers in the sample condense into Cooper pairs, the 
bound electron pairs whose macroscopic quantum coherence underpins the 
zero-resistance state~\cite{Tinkham1996}. The condensate carries supercurrent 
without dissipation, accounting for the complete resistivity suppression 
observed at the lowest measured temperatures. Above $T_{c,0}$, however, 
thermal fluctuations nucleate only transient Cooper pairs, producing a 
gradual suppression of resistance - a hallmark of 
paraconductivity~\cite{aslamazov1968,larkin2005}.

\ch{YBa2Cu3O7} contains quasi-two-dimensional CuO$_2$ layer structure and short coherence 
length~\cite{junod1990}, both of which amplify phase fluctuations relative 
to conventional BCS superconductors~\cite{Tinkham1996}. The observed 
transition widths $\Delta T_c \approx 17$--$19\,\text{K}$ 
(Table~\ref{tab:critical_temps_RvsT}) therefore reflect a combination of 
intrinsic critical fluctuations and the sample inhomogeneity.

As discussed in section \ref{sec:ac_susceptibility}, the area of the hysteresis curve is:

\begin{equation}
A_H = \oint \mu_0 H_\alpha \, dB = \int_{0}^{T} \mu_0 \left( H_0 + h_{AC} \cos(\omega t) \right) \frac{dB}{dt} \, dt
\end{equation}

$A_H$ itself is proportional to the energy loss $W_V$ per unit volume of the magnetic sample. 
The full equation for magnetic flux density $B= \mu_0 (H + M)$ is inserted into the previous equation. Since the magnetisation is a periodic function, it is represented as a Fourier series with just the first-order component here. After these substitutions, it is found that the $cos(\omega t)$ and $sin(\omega t)$ terms are integrated over the full time period $T=2\pi /\omega$ yielding a value of zero. It is found then that the energy loss is proportional to $\chi''$:

\begin{equation}
    A_H = \mu_0^2 \omega h_{AC} \int_{0}^{T} h_{AC} \chi'' \cos({\omega t})^2 \, dt = \mu_0^2 h_{AC}^2 \pi \chi''        
\end{equation}

$\chi''$ is proportional to $W_V$ if $h_{AC}$ is kept constant. \cite{experiment} 
\section{Levitation Experiments: Meissner Effect and Vortex Pinning}
In this experiment, a type II superconductor was used to perform a demonstration of the Meissner effect and the Shubnikov phase, along with vortex pinning. 

Graphite was initially used to get an idea of natural diamagnetism and we could observe the weak repulsion exhibited when it was placed on a magnetic track which can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:graphite}.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Photos lab day/20260513_170648.jpg}
    \caption{Graphite on a magnetic track showing weak repulsion due to its diamagnetic properties.}
    \label{fig:graphite}
\end{figure}

Ceramic samples were used for this experiment and they were covered in cotton and aluminium foil for better insulation. The sample was then placed in a pool of liquid nitrogen (with a boiling point of 77\,K) till it reached a low temperature below the critical temperature. The expulsion of the magnetic flux out the sample i.e. the Meissner effect was observed below the critical temperature.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Photos lab day/opposite poles.jpeg}
    \caption{Superconductor levitating stably in the weak-field pocket between two permanent magnet rings oriented with same poles facing each other. The repelling field geometry creates a local field minimum in the gap, allowing the Meissner effect to trap and stabilize the sample.}
    \label{fig:opposite poles}
\end{figure}
As shown in \textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:ring track}}, the sample was placed in the ring track where like poles of the magnets are opposite to each other. The sample levitates between the two rings and is stable when revolving around in the track. As the sample heats up, it loses the superconductivity and collapses when the temperature exceeds the critical temperature.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering \includegraphics[width=0.44\textwidth]{Photos lab day/circular track.jpg}
    \caption{Sample in motion over the magnetic ring track}
    \label{fig:ring track}
\end{figure}

In a type II superconductor, there is an influx of quantised magnetic field lines into the sample while in the Shubnikov phase and this results in some non-zero resistivity. These field lines are present as Abrikosov vortices that arrange themselves in a triangular lattice form. The \textquotedblleft{}flux-flow resistance\textquotedblright{} due to the movement of these vortices can be suppressed by defects in the sample. 

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.495\textwidth}
        \centering \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Photos lab day/vortex pinning.jpeg}
    \caption{Preparation of the sample for vortex pinning}
    \label{fig:vortex pin prep}
    \end{minipage}\hfill
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.495\textwidth}    
        \centering \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Photos lab day/levitation.jpeg}
        \caption{Levitation of sample after vortex pinning}
        \label{fig:vortex pin lev}
    \end{minipage}
\end{figure}

The sample is placed on the magnetic track, as seen in \textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:vortex pin prep}}, with a glass slide separating the two, and liquid nitrogen is slowly poured on it, until it cools down below the critical temperature. This pinning effect results in stronger superconductivity that allowed the sample to levitate even when the track was upside-down. This demonstrates how flux pinning locks the magnetic field lines within the superconductor, securing its spatial position on the track far more effectively than an unpinned or purely diamagnetic state.

\section[Conclusion]{Conclusion\protect}
Over the course of this experiment, the behaviour of the resistance and magnetic susceptibilities of the \ch{YBa2Cu3O7} sample have been studied. Properties of the superconductor like the Meissner effect and vortex pinning have been studied. 

\smallskip
Using the resistance-temperature curves, the transition onset temperature was found to be 127.88305 $\pm$ 0.47289 K while cooling and 125.31345 $\pm$ 0.36668 K while heating up. For cooling, the zero-resistance temperature was found to be 108.51967 $\pm$ 2.66596 K and 108.05284 $\pm$ 1.68423 K when heating.  Together, the curves exhibit hysteresis but the combined curve did not reflect a perfect sigmoidal shape. This could be attributed to spatial inhomogeneities in the sample and other factors like local strain causing distortion of the curve.

\smallskip
Next, the magnetic susceptibility-temperature measurements were done for both real and imaginary parts of the magnetic susceptibility. For the real part, the critical temperatures $T_{\text{c,0}}$ through cooling and heating were 93.27 K and 93.32 K, respectively. This was close to the accepted $T_{\text{c,0}}$ value of 93 K for the \ch{YBa2Cu3O7} sample. In addition, the changes in the magnetic properties of the material can be observed in the rapid descent from $T_{\text{c,onset}}$.  For the imaginary part, the heating and cooling curve mean temperatures were 99.0771 $\pm$ 0.01429 K and 92.7845 $\pm$ 0.005539 K. These curves also proved that the sample was a type II superconductor. 

\smallskip
Finally, small samples of ceramic were cooled in liquid nitrogen and turned into superconductors. These levitated over magnetic tracks along with stable motion, thus demonstrating the Meissner effect. Vortex pinning provided increased stability of motion and allowed the sample to counteract gravity even when the track was upside-down.
